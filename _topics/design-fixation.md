## Definition

**Design fixation** is an unintended and persistent restriction of design search caused by prior examples, features, knowledge, methods, or assumptions. It may appear as copying a flawed feature, repeatedly returning to one architecture, or failing to consider otherwise available regions of a design space.

> Our stance: fixation is not simply “having constraints” or “using precedent.” Expertise depends on both. Fixation is the failure to loosen a frame when the task calls for alternatives.

Examples can improve quality and feasibility while simultaneously narrowing variety. The important question is therefore not whether designers were influenced, but whether that influence became inappropriately restrictive.

## Stuck on examples

1. A designer encounters or retrieves an example, assumption, process, or familiar solution.
2. Salient features shape problem framing and candidate generation.
3. Search becomes concentrated around those features, sometimes without awareness.
4. Fixation is inferred by comparing feature copying, variety, novelty, process traces, or response to interventions.

Fixation can be semantic, functional, visual, architectural, or process-based. **Manufacturing fixation** occurs when familiar processes and their limits are treated as necessary even when a new process changes what is possible.

Our manufacturing-fixation studies sharpen that claim: designers can unconsciously cling not only to familiar processes but to the features those processes make familiar. Knowing that additive manufacturing permits something new is not the same as imagining with it. Prior manufacturing habits can limit the concepts that appear before feasibility is ever evaluated.

### Why examples cast long shadows
The concept is useful when diagnosing narrow ideation, designing example-based tools, teaching emerging manufacturing methods, studying analogy, or evaluating AI systems that may reproduce dominant patterns from training data.

The goal is not permanent divergence. Good design alternates between opening the space and committing with evidence.

### Getting unstuck without forgetting
- **Calling every repeated feature fixation.** Reuse can be rational; demonstrate inappropriate restriction.
- **Measuring idea count alone.** Many cosmetic variants can remain fixated on one architecture.
- **Assuming experts know when they are fixated.** Awareness and measured influence often diverge.
- **Removing examples entirely.** Varied, abstracted, and strategically timed examples can support better ideation.
- **Using “think outside the box” as a treatment.** Defixation needs concrete reframing or generative moves.

## Resources

- **[Design Fixation](https://doi.org/10.1016/0142-694X(91)90003-F)** — David Jansson and Steven Smith (1991), <em>Design Studies</em>. The foundational experiment showing that designers reproduce both helpful and flawed features from examples.
- **[Design and Other Types of Fixation](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0142-694X(96)00023-3)** — A. Terry Purcell and John Gero (1996), <em>Design Studies</em>. Situates design fixation among broader cognitive mechanisms and task conditions.
- **[A Study of Design Fixation, Its Mitigation and Perception in Engineering Design Faculty](https://doi.org/10.1115/1.4001110)** — Julie Linsey and colleagues (2010), <em>Journal of Mechanical Design</em>. Shows that expertise does not make designers immune and tests a mitigation intervention.
- **[Reducing and Perceiving Design Fixation](https://www.designsociety.org/publication/28853/)** — Design Society workshop paper and downloadable study context.
- **[Design Heuristics](https://www.designheuristics.com/)** — concrete transformations for moving beyond a dominant concept.
- **[On the Effective Use of Design-by-Analogy](https://www.designsociety.org/publication/30665/)** — examines when analogical examples expand or restrict ideation.
- **[Manufacturing Fixation in Design](https://doi.org/10.1115/1.4056222)** — **From our group.** defines and measures fixation on familiar manufacturing assumptions during idea generation.

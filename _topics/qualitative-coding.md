## Definition

**Qualitative coding** is the iterative process of assigning labels to meaningful segments of text, images, video, artifacts, or observations so that a researcher can compare cases, develop categories, examine patterns, and build an interpretation relevant to a research question.

> Our stance: coding is not highlighting, word counting, or converting interviews into cheap quantitative data. A code is an analytic claim about what a segment means in context.

Codes may be deductive, derived from theory or a prior framework; inductive, developed through engagement with the data; or abductive, refined through movement between surprising evidence and possible explanations. Most strong studies combine structure and emergence deliberately.

## Code of conduct

1. Define the research question, unit of analysis, and qualitative tradition.
2. Become familiar with the data and context before fragmenting it.
3. Assign initial codes to meaningful segments.
4. Compare segments and cases; split, merge, rename, and define codes.
5. Organize codes into categories, themes, processes, mechanisms, or theoretical propositions.
6. Return to disconfirming evidence, document decisions, and connect claims to excerpts.

A **codebook** typically records a code name, definition, inclusion and exclusion criteria, and examples. Multiple coders can support calibration or reliability in some traditions. In reflexive thematic analysis, however, disagreement is treated as interpretive material rather than error to eliminate. The epistemology should determine the quality criteria.

Automation changes the labor, not the epistemic responsibility. Our real-time Interaction Dynamics Notation system reached 80.3% agreement with human coding in a NASA design sprint—roughly comparable to human inter-rater reliability. That makes live communication diagnostics plausible. It does not make the machine's labels self-interpreting or turn agreement into truth.

### What coding makes discussable
Qualitative coding is useful when the question concerns meaning, process, experience, practice, interaction, or context—especially phenomena that predefined numerical measures would flatten. In design research it supports interview analysis, protocol studies, field observations, design-rationale analysis, and theory building.

It is not the best choice when the research question is only about frequency and the phenomenon is already validly measured.

### Rigor is not automation
- **Starting with software instead of a question.** NVivo, MAXQDA, Dedoose, and spreadsheets organize work; they do not supply an analysis.
- **Confusing topics with themes.** “Communication” is a topic; a theme makes a claim about how communication works in the data.
- **Treating intercoder agreement as universal rigor.** It is appropriate for some aims and incoherent for others.
- **Stripping excerpts from context.** Meaning depends on speaker, setting, sequence, and question.
- **Letting an LLM silently code the corpus.** Automation can assist comparison or retrieval, but researchers remain responsible for interpretation, privacy, and auditability.

## Resources

- **[The Coding Manual for Qualitative Researchers](https://us.sagepub.com/en-us/nam/the-coding-manual-for-qualitative-researchers/book273583)** — Johnny Saldaña (4th ed., 2021). A practical reference for first- and second-cycle coding methods.
- **[Using Thematic Analysis in Psychology](https://doi.org/10.1191/1478088706qp063oa)** — Virginia Braun and Victoria Clarke (2006), <em>Qualitative Research in Psychology</em>. The foundational account of thematic analysis and its analytic choices.
- **[Using Qualitative Research Methods in Engineering Design Research](https://www.designsociety.org/publication/34891/)** — Shanna Daly, Anna McGowan, and Panos Papalambros (2013). A Design Society introduction showing what qualitative methods reveal about complex design work.
- **[Thematic Analysis resources](https://www.thematicanalysis.net/)** — Braun and Clarke's guides, FAQs, and quality recommendations.
- **[Christina Harrington's research](https://www.christinaharrington.me/research-1)** — a strong model of qualitative, community-based, participatory design research centered on historically marginalized communities.
- **[James Holly Jr.'s faculty profile](https://stpp.fordschool.umich.edu/faculty/james-holly)** — pro-Black engineering-education scholarship that makes epistemic authority and analytic framing explicit.
- **[Analysing Design Protocols](https://www.designsociety.org/publication/32332/)** — an ontologically based coding approach for comparing design processes.
- **[Opportunities for Natural Language Processing in Qualitative Engineering Education Research](https://doi.org/10.1109/FIE.2018.8658747)** — **From our group.** shows how computational methods can augment rather than replace qualitative interpretation.
- **[A Real-Time Automatic Interaction Dynamics Notation Communication Analysis System](https://doi.org/10.1115/DETC2025-168665)** — **From our group.** compares automated coding of team interactions with human-coded communication data.

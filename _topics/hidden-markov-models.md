## Working definition

A **hidden Markov model (HMM)** is a probabilistic model for sequential data. At each time step, the system occupies a latent—or hidden—state. That state evolves according to a Markov chain and emits an observation according to a state-dependent probability distribution.

> The state is not observed directly; it must be inferred from the sequence of outputs it tends to produce.

An HMM separates two kinds of uncertainty: uncertainty about how the underlying state changes and uncertainty about what each state produces. For example, the hidden state might be a machine’s health condition while vibration measurements are the observations.

## How it works

A basic HMM contains:

1. **Hidden states:** a finite set of latent conditions.
2. **Initial probabilities:** a distribution over the first hidden state.
3. **Transition probabilities:** the probability of moving from one hidden state to another.
4. **Emission probabilities:** the probability distribution of observations conditioned on the current hidden state.

The standard model makes two conditional-independence assumptions. First, the next hidden state depends on the current hidden state rather than the full history. Second, the current observation depends on the current hidden state rather than directly on earlier states or observations.

Three computational problems organize most HMM use:

- **Evaluation:** How probable is an observed sequence under this model? The forward algorithm answers efficiently.
- **Decoding:** What hidden-state sequence best explains the observations? The Viterbi algorithm finds the most probable path; forward–backward methods provide state-by-state posterior probabilities.
- **Learning:** What transition and emission parameters best explain observed sequences? The Baum–Welch algorithm is an expectation-maximization procedure for local maximum-likelihood estimation.

## When it is useful

HMMs are useful when data arrive in sequence, regimes persist over time, and the regimes are not observed directly. Applications include speech recognition, biological sequence analysis, activity recognition, fault diagnosis, finance, weather regimes, and process monitoring.

They are most appropriate when a modest number of interpretable states and relatively simple dependence assumptions capture the important structure. Richer state-space models, conditional random fields, recurrent networks, or transformers may fit complex dependencies better, but often with different data, interpretability, and computational tradeoffs.

## Common mistakes

- **Confusing a hidden state with a label.** States are latent explanatory variables; their meanings are inferred and may not align cleanly with human categories.
- **Assuming the inferred path is certain.** Viterbi returns one most-probable joint path. It does not report the full posterior uncertainty.
- **Using too many states.** Additional states can absorb noise, become non-identifiable, or duplicate one another.
- **Ignoring duration behavior.** A standard HMM implies geometric state-duration distributions. Semi-Markov models are often better when dwell time matters.
- **Expecting Baum–Welch to find the global optimum.** Initialization matters because expectation-maximization can converge to local optima.

## Definition

**A team** is **three or more** interdependent people with a shared objective who must coordinate differentiated knowledge, roles, actions, or resources to succeed. A team has a meaningful boundary: it is possible to say who participates in the joint work and who does not.

> Our stance: this guide reserves “team” for three or more people and treats a pair as a dyad. Much of the literature says “two or more,” but dyads lack majority, coalition, and subgroup dynamics. That structural difference is too important to hide inside one word.

Not every collection is a team. A room of people receiving the same lecture is an audience. People whose individual outputs are merely pooled form a nominal group. A team exists when one member's work changes the conditions, information, or outcomes of others.

## There is an I in interdependence

Team performance emerges from several coupled layers:

1. **Composition:** knowledge, skill, experience, identity, cognitive style, and motivation.
2. **Structure:** roles, specialization, authority, subteams, and communication networks.
3. **Process:** coordination, information sharing, conflict, adaptation, and decision-making.
4. **Emergent states:** trust, shared mental models, psychological safety, cohesion, and collective efficacy.
5. **Context:** task structure, tools, incentives, time, organization, and environment.

These layers interact. There is no universally optimal team size, diversity profile, or meeting frequency; the right design depends on the problem.

That dependence is something our group models directly. Problem properties change which team size and interaction frequency perform best; communication that helps on one landscape can synchronize a team too early on another. “Build the best team” is incomplete. The design question is **best for what problem, under what coordination cost?**

Objective performance can also conceal a damaged team process. In our human–AI adaptation studies, hybrid teams recovered after a mid-task shock while showing weaker communication, coordination, and interpersonal evaluations. A team is not healthy merely because the artifact survived.

### When interdependence pays
Team language is useful when a task requires multiple forms of expertise, parallel effort, mutual adaptation, or robustness beyond one person. It is especially important in engineering because the technical architecture and team architecture often become coupled.

A team is unnecessary overhead when the work can be decomposed into independent individual tasks and recombined without coordination.

### More people is not more team
- **Calling any group a team.** Shared membership does not establish interdependence.
- **Assuming more people add capacity linearly.** Coordination channels and process loss grow quickly.
- **Treating diversity as a scalar good.** Differences help when the task needs them and the team can integrate them.
- **Optimizing composition while ignoring process.** A well-composed team can fail under poor information flow or incentives.
- **Using team-level conclusions from individual-level data.** Match claims and analysis to the level of the phenomenon.

## Resources

- **[Enhancing the Effectiveness of Work Groups and Teams](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1529-1006.2006.00030.x)** — Steve Kozlowski and Daniel Ilgen (2006), <em>Psychological Science in the Public Interest</em>. A comprehensive account of composition, processes, emergent states, and team effectiveness.
- **[Team Effectiveness 1997–2007](https://doi.org/10.1177/0149206308316061)** — John Mathieu and colleagues (2008), <em>Journal of Management</em>. Reviews a decade of evidence and influential input–mediator–outcome models.
- **[KABOOM: An Agent-Based Model for Simulating Cognitive Style in Team Problem Solving](https://doi.org/10.1017/dsj.2019.12)** — Samuel Lapp, Kathryn Jablokow, and Christopher McComb (2019), <em>Design Science</em>. Shows how composition, communication, specialization, and task structure can interact nonlinearly.
- **[Teams That Work: The Seven Drivers of Team Effectiveness](https://doi.org/10.1093/oso/9780190056964.001.0001)** — Scott Tannenbaum and Eduardo Salas translate the evidence into a practical account of capabilities, cooperation, coordination, communication, cognition, coaching, and conditions.
- **[DTM Past, Present, and Future](https://doi.org/10.1115/DETC2022-90003)** — reflections from and about the Design Theory and Methodology community.
- **[REID Lab](https://engineering.purdue.edu/reidlab/)** — Tahira Reid Smith's human-centered work is a model for treating people as core design-system elements rather than nuisance variables.
- **[Optimizing Design Teams Based on Problem Properties](https://doi.org/10.1115/1.4035793)** — **From our group.** demonstrates why team size and interaction frequency should be matched to the task.
- **[Focus and Modality](https://doi.org/10.1017/pds.2023.191)** — **From our group.** extends team design to human–AI collaboration through tool, partner, analytics, and coach roles.

## Working definition

**A dyad** is a social or collaborative unit containing **exactly two** interdependent members. The members may be two people, a human and an AI system, two organizations, or another pair of actors, but the unit is defined by a single reciprocal relationship.

> Our stance: a dyad is not a tiny team in this field guide. With two members there is no majority vote, coalition, mediator, or internal subgroup. If either member leaves, the dyad ceases to exist.

That structure makes dyads unusually intimate and fragile. Every contribution can be attributed to one of two actors, every interaction directly involves the only partner, and dependence is difficult to diffuse.

## How it works

Dyadic analysis separates several sources of variation:

1. **Actor effects:** how a member's characteristic influences their own outcome.
2. **Partner effects:** how that characteristic influences the other member's outcome.
3. **Relationship effects:** what is unique to this particular pairing.
4. **Reciprocity and time:** how each member's behavior changes the other's subsequent behavior.

Dyads can be distinguishable, such as teacher–student or human–AI, or exchangeable, such as two peers with the same role. Their observations are usually non-independent, so ordinary analyses that treat each person as isolated can be wrong.

## When it is useful

The dyad is the right unit for mentorship, interviews involving paired participants, pair programming, negotiation, close collaboration, human–AI partnership, and any study where reciprocal influence matters. It is also a useful baseline before adding the coalition and network dynamics that appear with three or more members.

## Common mistakes

- **Using “dyad” to mean any pair in a larger network.** Specify whether the pair is the unit or one edge within a larger system.
- **Treating observations as independent.** Partner outcomes often covary by construction.
- **Inferring team dynamics from dyads.** Adding a third member changes the social structure qualitatively.
- **Collapsing both members into one average.** Actor, partner, asymmetry, and relationship effects can disappear.
- **Calling a person and a passive tool a dyad.** Some mutual influence or relational interdependence should be present.

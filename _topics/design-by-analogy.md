## Definition

**Design by analogy** is the deliberate use of a source example to generate or understand a target design by mapping relationships, functions, or mechanisms between them. The essential act is structural mapping: “A relates to B in the source as X could relate to Y in the target.”

> Our stance: analogy is not visual copying and it is not merely “being inspired.” A useful analogy transfers a relationship or mechanism, then earns its place through adaptation and evaluation in the target domain.

Near-field analogies come from similar domains and are easier to retrieve. Far-field analogies cross domains and may support novelty, but the relevant mapping is harder to recognize. Biological analogy is one important subset, not the whole method.

## Same difference

1. Abstract the target problem in terms of functions, flows, behaviors, or relationships.
2. Retrieve source examples that share some of that relational structure.
3. Align source and target elements.
4. Transfer the mechanism or organizing principle—not every surface feature.
5. Adapt the idea to target constraints and test where the analogy breaks.

Tools such as WordTrees, patent search, functional models, and analogy repositories help retrieval. Representation matters: a functional description often exposes connections hidden by product names.

### When distance helps
Analogy is useful for conceptual design, reframing, mechanism generation, biomimicry, and translating knowledge across disciplines. It is particularly valuable when the current framing suggests only incremental changes.

It is dangerous when superficial similarity substitutes for causal evidence. An elegant analogy can still produce a poor design.

### Analogy is not resemblance
- **Copying appearance rather than relations.** Surface similarity is not the core of analogical transfer.
- **Stopping after retrieval.** Adaptation and evaluation do the design work.
- **Forcing every source feature into the target.** All analogies have limits.
- **Assuming far analogies are always better.** Distance can increase novelty and retrieval difficulty together.
- **Presenting an anecdote as validation.** Test the transferred mechanism under target conditions.

## Resources

- **[Structure-Mapping: A Theoretical Framework for Analogy](https://doi.org/10.1207/s15516709cog0702_3)** — Dedre Gentner (1983), <em>Cognitive Science</em>. The foundational account of analogy as mapping relational structure rather than object attributes.
- **[Design-by-Analogy: Experimental Evaluation of a Functional Analogy Search Methodology](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00163-014-0186-4)** — Katherine Fu and colleagues (2015), <em>Research in Engineering Design</em>. Tests functional retrieval strategies for engineering concept generation.
- **[Design by Analogy: A Study of the WordTree Method for Problem Re-Representation](https://doi.org/10.1115/1.4006145)** — Julie Linsey, Arthur Markman, and Kristin Wood (2012), <em>Journal of Mechanical Design</em>. Shows how linguistic re-representation can help designers retrieve analogies.
- **[Design-by-Analogy Using the WordTree Method](https://www.designsociety.org/publication/30694/)** — Julie Linsey and colleagues' practical retrieval approach.
- **[On the Effective Use of Design-by-Analogy](https://www.designsociety.org/publication/30665/)** — Katherine Fu and collaborators on distance, commonness, novelty, and fixation.
- **[AskNature](https://asknature.org/)** — a searchable resource for biologically inspired strategies; use it as a starting point, not proof of engineering performance.
- **[Co-Design Lab publications](https://codesign.berkeley.edu/papers/)** — Kosa Goucher-Lambert's research on inspiration retrieval and AI-enabled analogy tools.
